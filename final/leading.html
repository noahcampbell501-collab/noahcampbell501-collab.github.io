You are about to take 3-6 hours from around 4-6 peoples lives for weeks on end to tell your story. You are about to ask for more time than most churches do, so that they can have you tell them what to do and how to do it. They will probably spend money on real world materials so that they can improve in this game. They will donate their creativity and offer up their creations that they made with love to you. Hear these words.
FEAR THIS POWER

The only way you can have your power stripped away fom you is if you fail at your job, and the campaign dies. Believe me, that can happen fast. It could be a result of character flaw, but it can also be as easy as missing someone's miscommunicated expectations. Or it could be a lack of session planning. Or it can be drama between players. The life of a D&D campaign is as stable as midwest weather in April.

I have made an itemized list of campaign killers, organized in decending order of your fault to your innocents.

  1. Your player belittling self-absorbed railroading attention seeking face
    • As a Dungeon master, nothing can stop you in this game. But if you keep reminding people of that fact by making sure that they can't ever succeed and when they do it's because that's what the plot says, then you won't have a game for long.
    • Talk with them. Ask them how you can make the game more fun for them, if it's that important. I promise you, they won't feel blown away that they threw everything they had at their opponent, and it still did nothing. They're just going to get mad, and wait for you to bring in the new NPC you wrote to save them.
  2. Not scheduling
    • You can't play the game if you can't play the game. Even if your players are the hardest people in the world to get a hold of, it is your duty to wrangle them up. That is one of the most important parts about this position, and without it, you will lose respect from your players almost instantly.
  3. Party drama
    • If someone is acting like a jerk under your roof and at your table, call them out on it. You are the authority figure here, and reminding them that this is a game is what you should do as a friend regardless. If it's drama outside the table... then you get a pass
  4. The players lose interest in the game
    • sshhhh. It's OK, baby. Let it out. I know it hurts. It's not your fault. They just had other interest. It's not you. You did a good job. You are beautiful and strong. You are a skilled metal worker. But I know it sucks. You'll be OK. You'll find another group to play with.
